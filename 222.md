A company operates quick-service restaurants. The restaurants follow a predictable model with high sales traffic for 4 hours daily. Sales traffic is lower outside of those peak hours.
The point of sale and management platform is deployed in the AWS Cloud and has a backend that is based on Amazon DynamoDB. The database table uses provisioned throughput mode with 100,000 RCUs and 80,000 WCUs to match known peak resource consumption.
The company wants to reduce its DynamoDB cost and minimize the operational overhead for the IT staff.
Which solution meets these requirements MOST cost-effectively?

A. Reduce the provisioned RCUs and WCUs.
B. Change the DynamoDB table to use on-demand capacity.
C. Enable Dynamo DB auto scaling for the table.
D. Purchase 1-year reserved capacity that is sufficient to cover the peak load for 4 hours each day.


Suggest Answer: C

---

## 📊 Analysis

### 🔍 Understanding the Problem

The company operates quick-service restaurants with a **predictable traffic pattern**: 4 hours of high sales traffic daily, with lower traffic during the remaining 20 hours. Their DynamoDB table is configured with **provisioned throughput mode** at 100,000 RCUs and 80,000 WCUs — set to match peak consumption. This means during off-peak hours, they are paying for unused throughput capacity. The goal is to **reduce DynamoDB cost** while **minimizing operational overhead** for IT staff.

### ✅ Correct Option: C — Enable DynamoDB Auto Scaling for the Table

**DynamoDB auto scaling** uses **AWS Application Auto Scaling** to dynamically adjust the provisioned read and write capacity units based on actual workload utilization. It works by:

- Monitoring CloudWatch metrics (`ConsumedReadCapacityUnits`, `ConsumedWriteCapacityUnits`)
- Scaling up when utilization exceeds the target threshold (e.g., 70%)
- Scaling down when utilization drops below the target threshold

This is the **most cost-effective** solution for a predictable workload because:
- During the 4-hour peak, auto scaling will automatically provision sufficient RCUs/WCUs (up to the configured maximum)
- During the remaining 20 off-peak hours, it will scale down to a much lower baseline, dramatically reducing costs
- **Zero operational overhead** — no manual intervention, no cron jobs, no Lambda functions to adjust throughput
- It combines the cost predictability of provisioned mode with the flexibility of automatic scaling

### ❌ Incorrect Options

**A. Reduce the provisioned RCUs and WCUs**
- Simply lowering the provisioned throughput would cause **throttling** and `ProvisionedThroughputExceededException` errors during the 4-hour peak window
- This directly impacts the point-of-sale system during the busiest hours — unacceptable for a live restaurant operation
- Does not address the core need: the system MUST handle peak load

**B. Change the DynamoDB table to use on-demand capacity**
- On-demand mode charges **per-request** and is optimized for **unpredictable** or unknown workloads
- For a workload with a well-understood, predictable 4-hour peak, on-demand can be **more expensive** than provisioned mode with auto scaling at high throughput levels
- On-demand has no concept of "reserved capacity" discount; provisioned + auto scaling + reserved capacity yields better cost outcomes
- On-demand also has initial throughput limits that may require requesting a service quota increase for 100K+ RCU/WCU workloads

**D. Purchase 1-year reserved capacity that is sufficient to cover the peak load for 4 hours each day**
- Reserved capacity provides a **billing discount** on provisioned throughput — it does NOT automatically adjust capacity
- You would still be provisioning 100,000 RCUs and 80,000 WCUs 24/7, just at a discounted rate
- This is suboptimal because you're still paying for unused capacity during 20 hours daily (albeit at a discount)
- Reserved capacity works best when **combined with** auto scaling: purchase reserved capacity for the baseline, and let auto scaling handle peaks above that baseline

### 🏭 Enterprise Hands-On Knowledge

1. **Auto Scaling Target Utilization**: The default target is 70%. For cost-sensitive workloads, you can set this to 80-90% to minimize over-provisioning. For latency-sensitive workloads, set it lower (50-60%) to maintain headroom.

2. **Combining Reserved Capacity with Auto Scaling**: The enterprise best practice is to purchase reserved capacity for your **baseline** (off-peak) throughput, and rely on auto scaling to handle the delta up to peak. This maximizes both cost savings and performance.

3. **Scale-Up Cooldown**: DynamoDB auto scaling has a default scale-up cooldown of 0 seconds (immediate) and scale-down cooldown of 300 seconds (5 minutes). For restaurants' 4-hour peak, this means the system will react quickly to the lunch/dinner rush.

4. **CloudWatch Alarm Integration**: Each scaling policy creates CloudWatch alarms (one for scale-up, one for scale-down). Be aware of CloudWatch alarm costs if you have hundreds of tables — each table with auto scaling creates 4 alarms (2 for reads, 2 for writes).

5. **Auto Scaling vs. On-Demand Decision Matrix**:
   | Factor | Provisioned + Auto Scaling | On-Demand |
   |--------|---------------------------|-----------|
   | Predictable workload | ✅ Best | ❌ Overpriced |
   | Unpredictable/spiky | ❌ May throttle during spikes | ✅ Best |
   | Cost at high throughput | ✅ Lower with reserved | ❌ Higher per-request cost |
   | Operational overhead | ✅ Automatic | ✅ Automatic |
   | New table with unknown traffic | ❌ Requires tuning | ✅ Best initial choice |

6. **Monitoring**: Use `ThrottledRequests` CloudWatch metric to verify that auto scaling is keeping up. Set up alarms on this metric to catch scaling issues before they affect customers.
